Combining strengthening, flexibility and aerobic exercise (either on land or in water) will help to better manage knee osteoarthrtis (1).
As pain may be too severe to carry out land-based activities, aquatic exercises should be carried out initially, with the aim of improvng physical ability, so land-based exercises can then be performed (2).
Improved pain and function if exercises that improve the three aforemetioned components of fitness (strength, flexibility and aerobic) are carried out (1). Aquatic exercise has been found to reduce pain in the short term, but less significant benefits were found in the longer term, which is why land-based exercise should be carried out when possible (N.B aquatic exercise is more commonly known as hydotherapy i.e. undertaking exercises in water maintained between 32-36 degrees centigrade) (2).
A review of all the literature since 2002 concluded that said exercises are benefitial to patients with knee osteoarthtritis, and it is unlikley that any future trials will fail to support this. The effect of balance/relaxation exercises such as t'ai chi were not investigated in this study.

Prehabilitation (peoperative exercise intervention)
Currently severe osteoarthritis of the hip/knee is treated with a total joint replacement (1). The aim of prehabiliation is to improve the strength of muscles, tendons and ligaments surrounding the osteoarthritic joint prior to a total joint replacement, in order to enhance post-surgery rehabilitation and functional ability (2). Most of the programmes that have been tested thus far comprise three components; resistance training, flexibility, and step training, and have been completed by the patients three times a week approx 1-2 months before surgery (2,3). Results from pilot clinical trials have been very promising, and indicate that prehabilitation can improve the quality of life of people who undergo joint replacement becuase of severe osteoathritis. Findings of studies include improved quadriceps strength, functional ability (better performace at several functional tasks, such as walking up strairs, when compared with patients who did not undergo perhabilitation) 1 and 3 months after surgery (1,2,3,4) and reduced pain (2).

There is significant support for prehabiliation in the literature, due to promising results in trials. While prehabilitation has almost unanimously been found to improve strength and function, pain reduction has less frequentlybeen reported. An explaination for this may be that a longer prehabilitation period (>2 months), and hence stronger muscles, is required in order to see significant improvements in pain.
