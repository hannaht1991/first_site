Combining strengthening, flexibility and aerobic exercise (either on land or in the water) will help to better manage knee osteoarthrtis.
Improved pain and function if exercises working on all 3 aspects mentioned (strength, flexibility and aerobic) are carried out (1). Aquatic exercise has been found to reduce pain in the short term, but less significant benefits were found in the longer term e.g. carry out aquatic exercises initially, which hopefully should improve physical ability (pain and function), so land-based activities can begin (N.B aquatic exercise is more commonly known as hydotherapy i.e. undertaking exercises in water maintained between 32-36 degrees centigrade) (2).
A review of all the literature since 2002 has been analysed, and it was concluded that said exercises are benefitial to patients with knee osteoarthtritis - it is highly unlikely that results from any future trials will fail to support this. They didn't in this study, however, investigate the effect of balance/relaxation exercises such as t'ai chi.
1. Uthman, O. A. et al. Exercise for lower limb osteoarthritis: systematic review incorporating trial sequential analysis and network meta-analysis. BMJ 347, f5555 (2013). 2. Bartels, E. M. et al. Aquatic exercise for the treatment of knee and hip osteoarthritis. Cochrane Database Syst Rev CD005523 (2007).
